Research

At large, I’m interested in how systems neuroscience (including computational models that combine physiology with system variability) scales up/explains higher-order cognitive phenomena like memory, decision-making, and learning.

At the Losonczy Lab, I work on a project, advised by Abhi Shah, that investigates how CA1 organization affects representations and behavior outputs in the hippocampus. I developed Python-based pipelines to process and analyze data from behavioral recordings and neural activity from two-photon calcium imaging experiments. I also perform stereotaxic surgeries (such as viral injections, headpost, and cannula implantations) as well as train mice daily on a virtual reality (VR) based decision-making task. With this, I design and implement various cued and stimulation-centered VR paradigms, assess their effectiveness via pipelines, and iterate experimental designs accordingly. of

At the Bath Lab, I contribute to studies examining the intergenerational effects of early life adversity on maternal care and offspring outcomes. I use EthoVision XT to score detailed maternal behaviors — such as nursing posture, kicking, and circling — helping quantify behavioral patterns critical to understanding developmental trajectories in rodent models.

I'm drawn to CogSci because of its foundations in the philosophy of science. Through coursework in philosophy, linguistics, and neuroscience, I've come to adopt to hold a moderate empiricist view of cognition: while we may begin with certain innate structures or primitives (for ex, the concept of an 'agent'), these are scaffolded into relevant representations through experience and sensory input.
Some relevant papers I've written for courses:

Cognitive Flexibility Through Context-Dependent Memory Encoding: How Latent Task Structures Drive State Orthogonalization in the Hippocampus
Paper written for PHIL 3655: Topics in Cognitive Science and Philosophy, taught by Dr. Brendan Fleig-Goldstein, Spring 2025.
This paper situates a 2025 study on cognitive maps and orthogonalization in the hippocampus within Cameron Buckner's theory of Domain General Modular Architecture. Through this exploration, I advocate for a restructuring of our understanding of memory’s function and organization, not as a passive repository of sensory inputs, but as a dynamic system that constructs task-relevant distinctions between overlapping experiences.
(Link)

Image Quantization via k-means Clustering for Analyzing Oil Spill Data
Group project writeup written for MATH 2015: Linear Algebra & Probability, taught by Dr. Evan Sorenson, Fall 2024.
This report discusses an application of image quantization, via a k-means clustering algorithm, to analyze a dataset of oil spill satellite images and evaluate the effectiveness of this method (for data reduction) in accurately computing the percentage of oil spills. Our findings explore the limitations of k-means clustering in the analysis of dynamic datasets that have feature and environmental variability. (Link)
